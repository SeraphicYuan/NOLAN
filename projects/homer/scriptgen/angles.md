# Candidate angles — Homer / The Odyssey

**Inferred spine type (from the style guide):** a *central human/thematic CONTRAST*, braided the whole way through with the author's real life — "biographical determinism," where each life-fact is cashed out as a specific element of the work. The channel's videos are built on one felt opposition (Dodgson vs. Carroll; idealism vs. betrayal; math vs. fantasy) carried by the author's biography, closing on mystery/"unknowing" rather than a tidy verdict.

**The Homer problem, stated plainly:** this channel's one non-negotiable move — unlock the fiction with the author's life — has almost nothing to unlock. Homer has no reliable biography; he may not be one person. So the *right-type* angle must convert that void into the contrast, honestly, without inventing a life. The forgery/"who wrote it" debate is grounding + a possible hook — NOT the spine (a dry authenticity argument is exactly the low-resonance, wrong-type material the task says to penalize).

---

### Angle 1 — The homecoming poem written by the one Greek who never comes home to us  **[CHOSEN]**
- *thesis:* The Odyssey is the greatest story of a man fighting his way home — and its author is a figure we can never reach: a blind singer, maybe not even one man, forever just over the horizon. What Ithaca is to Odysseus, Homer is to us.
- *core (the contrast):* the wanderer who finally gets home vs. the poet who never does — presence vs. absence, sight vs. blindness. And the poem knows it: in Book 8 it hands us its own self-portrait, the blind bard Demodocus, whose *song* is more real to the room than the living man singing it.
- *supported by:* [S1,S4] Demodocus + blindness; [S3,S4] the unresolved Homeric Question; [S1] the recognition/homecoming scenes to cash the contrast against.
- *why a viewer cares:* everyone knows the ache of a home — or a person — that stays just out of reach; this makes the "we can't find Homer" problem *feel* like the poem's own subject instead of a footnote.
- **Resonance 5/5** · **Evidence 4/5** · **Style-fit 5/5**
- *Why it wins:* it is a genuine human contrast (the guide's spine type), and it solves biographical determinism the only honest way Homer allows — the missing life becomes the theme, and the poem literally contains the author's mirror (Demodocus). It demotes the forgery debate to a hook + one accelerated fact-cluster, and it lands naturally on the guide's signature "unknowing" close.

### Angle 2 — Cunning over strength: the survival of the man who wasn't strongest
- *thesis:* In a world that worshipped Achilles' brute glory, Homer built his second epic around the opposite hero — the man who wins by wits, patience, and lies, and who chooses to *live*.
- *core:* metis (cunning) vs. bia (force); the "Nobody" trick, the mast, the bed — intelligence as heroism.
- *supported by:* [S1,S2] Cyclops/Sirens; [S4] Achilles' shade ("rather a live serf than king of the dead"), Athena-as-wits.
- *why a viewer cares:* it flatters the reader's own kind of strength — brains and endurance over muscle.
- **Resonance 4/5** · **Evidence 5/5** · **Style-fit 4/5**
- *Note:* excellent and on-type, but the author-braid is thin (we'd lean on "blindness → survival by inner sight," which Angle 1 already absorbs). Its best material folds into the chosen angle.

### Angle 3 — The ache of nostos: home, fidelity, and the long way back
- *thesis:* The Odyssey is really about *homesickness* — a husband, a wife, and a son held apart for twenty years by war, gods, and time.
- *core:* wandering vs. home; Penelope's faith mirroring Odysseus's endurance; xenia as the thread that carries a stranger home.
- *supported by:* [S1,S2] Penelope's shroud, the bed; [S4] xenia, the Agamemnon/Clytemnestra dark mirror.
- *why a viewer cares:* the most universal feeling in the poem — longing for home and for the people in it.
- **Resonance 5/5** · **Evidence 5/5** · **Style-fit 3/5**
- *Note:* deeply resonant but the biographical braid is nearly impossible (whose life mirrors Penelope?). The chosen angle keeps this emotional core in its back third without needing a biography it can't supply.

### Angle 4 — Sung, not written: the poem that proves a "flaw" is genius
- *thesis:* The repetitions and "epithets" that look like clumsiness are the fingerprints of a poem composed *in performance*, from memory, before writing.
- *core:* oral tradition vs. our idea of "a book"; formulas reframed as the machinery of memory.
- *supported by:* [S3,S4] oral-formulaic theory, Yugoslav singers, epithets; [S4] the 24-book division.
- *why a viewer cares:* it changes how you *hear* the poem.
- **Resonance 3/5** · **Evidence 5/5** · **Style-fit 2/5**
- *Penalized:* mechanism-forward and lecture-prone — risks the isolated analysis-only beat the guide forbids. Its payload (formulas, oral tradition) is *grounding* and belongs inside Angle 1's context cluster, not as the spine.

### Angle 5 — Is Homer real? The 200-year forgery trial
- *thesis:* Wolf, Schliemann, Ventris, and today's online "pseudo-history" — did Homer even exist?
- *core:* real vs. fake.
- *supported by:* [S3] Wolf 1795, Schliemann 1873, Calvert, Ventris 1952.
- **Resonance 2/5** · **Evidence 5/5** · **Style-fit 1/5**
- *Penalized (hard):* this is exactly the dry "is it real / who wrote it" debate the task warns against for a human-thematic channel. It is superb *grounding* and a genuine *hook*, but as a spine it would produce a debate video, not a Great-Books-Explained essay. Used as hook + accelerated fact-cluster inside the chosen angle.

---

**Winner: Angle 1.** It wins on **resonance** (the longing for the unreachable — home, and the poet — is the most felt thing in the whole corpus) and on **style-fit** (it is a true human contrast, delivers biographical determinism honestly via Demodocus, absorbs the best of Angles 2–3 as its body and Angles 4–5 as grounding, and ends on the guide's beloved "unknowing").
