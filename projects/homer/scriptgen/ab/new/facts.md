- Seven cities claimed Homer as their citizen — src:[S4] · purpose:hook · beat:hook · conf:claimed · role:context

- Later Greek sources give only a few unreliable scraps about the poet Homer — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- Whether Homer was a single historical person is unresolved — scholars call this "the Homeric Question" — src:[S3] · purpose:hook · beat:hook · conf:disputed · role:challenges
- The story and many lines were handed down to Homer by generations of oral poets before being written — src:[S4][S3] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- In 1795 the German scholar F. A. Wolf ("Introduction to Homer") argued the Iliad and Odyssey were not composed at one stroke by one poet but accreted from many scattered oral songs, later edited into fixed versions — src:[S3] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- Wolf stressed that early Greece may have lacked a mature writing system able to preserve texts of such length — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:claimed · role:challenges
- Wolf's argument turned Homer from an object of worship into a subject of modern scholarship — the origin of the "Homeric Question" — src:[S3] · purpose:transition · beat:context · conf:verified · role:context
- Even before Wolf, critical historians no longer read the Iliad as a reliable historical record — divine intervention, prophecy, poetic embellishment marked it as myth/imagination — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:context

## The forgery charge (challenges — S3's counter material)

- A strand of Chinese-internet "pseudo-history" (伪史论) claims the Homeric epics are a later forgery, part of a fabricated Western history — src:[S3] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:disputed · role:challenges
- Forgery proponents argue that because the West cannot prove Homer really existed, the epics must be fakes "attributed to the ancients" — src:[S3] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:disputed · role:challenges

## The rebuttal / authenticity evidence (supports)

- The epics are written in a special "epic dialect" mixing forms from different regions and eras — evidence of long oral layering, not a single forger at a desk — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- Fixed epithets and repeated formulas ("swift-footed Achilles," "resourceful Odysseus") are memory-and-rhythm tools of oral composition, not clumsy flaws — src:[S3][S4] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- The "oral-formulaic theory" — drawn from studying still-living oral singers — showed such poets recompose within traditional formulas and frames rather than memorize one fixed text — src:[S3][S4] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- Comparable living oral poets, singing without written notes poems as long as the Iliad, were recorded in early-20th-century former Yugoslavia — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- The Odyssey contains its own portrait of an oral poet: the blind singer Demodocus, who performs the famous deeds of men at the Phaeacian court — src:[S4][S1][S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Heinrich Schliemann — a wealthy merchant obsessed with Homer since childhood who taught himself Ancient Greek — excavated Hisarlik in NW Turkey, believing the Iliad preserved real historical memory — src:[S3] · purpose:humanize · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- The Hisarlik site had first been identified by British diplomat Frank Calvert, who persuaded Schliemann — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:context
- Schliemann cut a huge trench (~40 m wide, 17 m deep) through the site, causing great destruction, and in 1873 announced "Priam's Treasure" — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:context
- Later, more careful archaeology confirmed Hisarlik corresponds to the traditional site of Troy — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- Archaeology cannot prove Achilles killed Hector or that the Wooden Horse happened, but it shows a real geographic and historical backdrop behind the poem — the epics sit between myth, literature, and historical memory — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- Linear B tablets were found by Arthur Evans at the palace of Knossos on Crete, and later at Pylos, Mycenae and Tiryns — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:context
- In 1952 Michael Ventris deciphered Linear B as an early (Mycenaean) form of Greek — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- Linear B proves the Bronze-Age Greek world already had writing, palaces and administration — refuting the "no writing, therefore forgery" claim (though the tablets are palace inventories, not literary manuscripts) — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- S3's analogy: China's own Dream of the Red Chamber has rival manuscript systems and a disputed final 40 chapters, yet no one calls it a forgery — textual variance is not fabrication — src:[S3] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports

## Craft & structure (supports)

- The Odyssey was divided into 24 books to match the 24 letters of the Greek alphabet — about 400 years after composition, not by Homer — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- The poem also falls into six movements of four books each — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:claimed · role:context
- Formulas work at every scale: epithet+noun ("rosy-fingered Dawn," "grey-eyed Athene"), repeated typical scenes, and recurring themes (the disguised guest, the returning stranger, sacrifice) — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- The poem's very first word is "man"; yet we do not meet Odysseus until book five — a deliberate aesthetic of retardation and expansion — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Odysseus is the "man of many turns / many wiles" — a hero defined by disguise, borrowed names and retold tales (Greek epithet *polytropos*) — src:[needs-check] · purpose:proof · beat:hook · conf:claimed · role:supports

## Plot & cast (evidence)

- His journey home took ten years; of twelve ships and hundreds of men, none survived — src:[S1][S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports

## The monsters as first-person testimony (hidden detail — supports)

- The fantastic monsters — Cyclops, Circe, Sirens, Scylla, Charybdis — appear only in books 9–12, about a sixth of the poem — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Those four books are narrated by Odysseus himself, creating a different register set against the realism of the rest of the poem — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- In his own tale Odysseus appears less admirable — foolish to reveal his name to the Cyclops, over-lingering with Circe — hinting the teller flatters himself — src:[S4] · purpose:contrast · beat:evidence · conf:claimed · role:supports

## Theme, meaning, close (turn / close)

- The Odyssey sets Agamemnon (murdered by unfaithful Clytemnestra and her lover Aegisthus) against Odysseus (saved by faithful Penelope) — a study of two homecomings — src:[S4] · purpose:contrast · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- Penelope matches Odysseus in cunning: she unwove the shroud each night to stall the suitors, and tested him with the secret of the immovable olive-tree bed — src:[S1][S2] · purpose:proof · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- Odysseus and Penelope embody "like-mindedness" as the ideal of marriage — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports (Greek term *homophrosyne* — src:[needs-check] · conf:claimed)
- In the underworld Achilles' shade says he would rather be a landless man's slave, alive, than king over all the dead — the Odyssey prizes life and home where the Iliad prized glory — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- Guest-friendship (xenia) is a central organizing theme: how you treat strangers defines civilization — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- The old Greeks already ranked Homer among the greatest poets; Romans, medieval scholars and Renaissance humanists kept returning to him — the epics carried education, rhetoric, ethics and cultural memory — src:[S3] · purpose:authority · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- The returning-hero quest runs from Beowulf and El Cid to the modern road movie — and S3 notes superhero arcs (Iron Man, Batman, Spider-Man) repeat the same frame — src:[S4][S3] · purpose:proof · beat:close · conf:claimed · role:supports
- Over 100 suitors infested Odysseus's palace; he strung the great bow, shot through 12 axe-heads, and killed them all — homecoming is also blood — src:[S1][S2] · purpose:contrast · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
