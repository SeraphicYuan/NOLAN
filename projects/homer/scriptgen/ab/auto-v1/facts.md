## Beat: HOOK  (the poet we cannot find / the shock)

- Seven cities in the ancient world each claimed Homer as their own citizen — src:[S4] · purpose:hook · beat:hook · conf:verified · role:context
- Homer is said to have been blind — but "the richness of his visual imagery renders this doubtful" — src:[S4] · purpose:contrast · beat:hook · conf:disputed · role:challenges
- A recent strand of online "pseudo-history" (伪史论) claims the Homeric epics are outright later forgeries and that Homer never existed — src:[S3] · purpose:hook · beat:hook · conf:claimed · role:challenges
- The reality scholars actually hold is messier than "real vs. fake": the poems sit "between myth, literature, and historical memory" — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:hook · conf:verified · role:supports
- In Book 8 of the Odyssey a BLIND singer, Demodocus, performs — "the Muse took his eyes but gave him the sweet singing art" — a poet who looks like Homer's self-portrait inside his own poem — src:[S1,S4] · purpose:hook · beat:hook · conf:verified · role:supports

## Beat: CONTEXT / THE "BIOGRAPHY" THAT ISN'T  (author + oral tradition)

- Homer is dated to around 700 BC — roughly 2,800 years ago — src:[S1,S2,S4] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- He is credited with two epics that became "the foundation of Western literature": the Iliad (the siege of Troy) and the Odyssey (Odysseus's homecoming) — src:[S1,S4] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- The story and "many of the lines" were handed to Homer "by generations of oral poets" — the poem was sung long before it was written — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- Living oral poets who sang works as long as the Iliad or Odyssey, from memory and without written notes, were still recorded in the former Yugoslavia in the early 20th century — the field evidence that such feats are humanly possible — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- The scholarly account of that memory-machinery is the "oral-formulaic theory" (口头程式理论): singers don't recite a fixed text, they re-compose within traditional formulas — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- The theory's founders were Milman Parry and his student Albert Lord — src:[needs-check] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:claimed · role:supports
- The tell-tale formulas: fixed epithets like "swift-footed Achilles" and "resourceful Odysseus" (捷足的阿喀琉斯 / 足智多谋的奥德修斯) — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- And "Dawn with her rosy fingers" and "gray-eyed Athena" — repeated lines the singer leaned on to build the poem in performance — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- "The wine-dark sea" — the most famous of these recurring epithets — src:[needs-check] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:claimed · role:supports
- The Odyssey's first word is not Odysseus's name but "man" — "Tell me of the man…"; we don't even meet him until Book 4 (deliberate retardation/expansion) — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- The Greek word behind it is *polytropos* — "the man of many turns / many wiles" — src:[needs-check] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:claimed · role:supports

## Beat: EVIDENCE  (the wanderer + the ground under the myth)

- The Odyssey was later divided into 24 books, one for each of the 24 letters of the Greek alphabet — a split made ~400 years after composition, not by Homer — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Scholars note a more organic division: six parts of four books each — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:context
- The fantastical monsters (Cyclops, Circe, Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis) appear ONLY in Books 9–12 — about a sixth of the poem — and only inside the story Odysseus tells about himself — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Odysseus escapes the Cyclops Polyphemus by giving his name as "Nobody," blinding the giant with a sharpened stake, then riding out under the sheep — src:[S1,S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Polyphemus is a son of Poseidon; Odysseus's boast of his true name lets the Cyclops curse him — hence Poseidon's decade of revenge — src:[S1,S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Athena, goddess of wisdom, protects Odysseus throughout; his help from her is often indistinguishable from his own quick wits — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- The hero survives the Sirens by having his crew plug their ears with wax while he is bound to the mast — the only man to hear the song and live — src:[S1,S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Odysseus loses every one of his 12 ships and all his men; the comrades die by their OWN fault (opening the wind-bag; eating the Sun-god's cattle) — src:[S1,S4] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- His crew's fatal transgression: slaughtering the cattle of the sun-god Helios, against explicit warning — Zeus destroys the ship with a thunderbolt — src:[S1,S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- The real ground under the myth: Heinrich Schliemann excavated at Hisarlik in Turkey and in 1873 unearthed gold he called "Priam's treasure," suggesting Troy was a real place — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- But Schliemann did not find the site alone — the British-born diplomat Frank Calvert had identified Hisarlik first and persuaded him — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:context
- Schliemann's huge "trench" (~40m wide, ~17m deep) cut through the site's center and destroyed a great deal in the process — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:context
- Clay tablets in an unknown script (Linear B) were found by Arthur Evans at Knossos on Crete; Michael Ventris deciphered them in 1952, proving they recorded an early Greek — Mycenaean Greek — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- So the Bronze-Age world the poem remembers was NOT a blank, writing-less void — it had palaces, administration, and a writing system — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports

## Beat: TURN  (the mirror: the poet inside the poem / the Homeric Question)

- In 1795 the German scholar F. A. Wolf published his *Prolegomena ad Homerum* (《荷马导论》), arguing the epics were not written whole by one man but accreted from an older oral tradition — this launched the 200-year "Homeric Question" — src:[S3] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:challenges
- Wolf's move turned Homer "from a traditional object of worship into an object of modern scholarship" — src:[S3] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:challenges
- The special "epic language" of the poems mixes dialects and words from different eras — internal proof of long layering, not a single desk-forgery — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- On the Phaeacian island, Odysseus — still hiding his name — listens to the blind bard Demodocus sing the fall of Troy and Odysseus's own deeds, and weeps into his cloak — src:[S1,S2,S4] · purpose:humanize · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- The channel's usual key is "the author's life unlocks the fiction." Here the author's life is unknowable — so the poem hands us its self-portrait instead: the blind singer whose song is more real to the room than the living man in it — src:[S1,S4] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:claimed · role:supports
- S3's core correction: version-complexity is not forgery. Chinese classics (e.g. *Dream of the Red Chamber*, with its rival manuscript systems) face the same textual puzzles without being called fakes — "acknowledging complexity is scholarship; collapsing it into 'all fake' is conspiracy" — src:[S3] · purpose:authority · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports

## Beat: CLOSE  (recognition, homecoming, unknowing)

- Odysseus is recognized at home not by his face but by hidden signs: the old nurse Eurycleia knows the boar-scar on his thigh as she washes his feet — src:[S1] · purpose:humanize · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- Penelope tests him with the secret only they share — their marriage bed, which Odysseus built around a living olive tree rooted in the ground and cannot be moved — src:[S1] · purpose:humanize · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- The homecoming took twenty years — ten of war, ten of wandering — and Penelope stayed faithful, stalling 100+ suitors for three years by weaving and secretly un-weaving a shroud — src:[S1,S2] · purpose:proof · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- Odysseus is set against Agamemnon: the king who came home fast and was murdered by an unfaithful wife (Clytemnestra) and her lover — the dark mirror of the slow, cunning survivor met by a faithful wife — src:[S4] · purpose:contrast · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- In the underworld, the ghost of Achilles tells Odysseus he would rather be a landless serf alive than king over all the dead — the poem's verdict that survival and home outrank glory (its answer to the Iliad) — src:[S4] · purpose:authority · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- The Odyssey's declared "message": the trials of the journey — which test his character and courage — become more important than the reason for the trip or even the arrival itself — src:[S4] · purpose:authority · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports
- Its central recurring theme is *xenia*, guest-friendship — how strangers are treated, from Polyphemus who eats his guests to the hospitality that finally carries Odysseus home — src:[S4] · purpose:proof · beat:close · conf:verified · role:context
- The quest-home arc of the Odyssey seeds everything after it — "from Beowulf to El Cid to the modern road movie," the hero's journey home is a template for every generation — src:[S4] · purpose:authority · beat:close · conf:verified · role:supports

## Challenges / steelman material (kept deliberately)

- "Homer" may be no single man at all but a name for a centuries-long tradition of singers — the Homeric Question is genuinely unresolved — src:[S3,S4] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:disputed · role:challenges
- The blindness is probably legend, not fact — the visual imagery argues against it — src:[S4] · purpose:contrast · beat:hook · conf:disputed · role:challenges
- Archaeology can locate Troy but cannot prove the Trojan War, the wooden horse, or that Achilles killed Hector happened as sung — src:[S3] · purpose:contrast · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:challenges
