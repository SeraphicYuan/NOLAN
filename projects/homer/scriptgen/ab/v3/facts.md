# Grounded Facts — Homer / The Odyssey (v3)

Taxonomy per claim: `src` · `purpose` · `beat` · `conf` (high/med/low) · `role` (supports = advances the chosen homecoming spine · challenges = the forgery/authorship counter-material · context = grounding).

The chosen spine is **the human longing to come home and be *known*** (home over glory), with the biographical-mirroring move *redirected* onto the vanished author. So the S3 forgery material is filed as **challenges** (a hook + one accelerated fact-cluster), never as the thesis.

## A. The author & the "who was Homer" material (hook + redirected-mirroring cluster)

1. A blind poet known as Homer produced two epics — the *Iliad* (siege of Troy) and the *Odyssey* (the journey home) — the foundation of Western literature. · src:[S1][S2][S4] · purpose:anchor subject · beat:Hook/Thesis · conf:high · role:context
2. Dated to roughly 700 BC / "2,800 years ago"; Homer was *said* to be blind, though the visual richness of the verse "renders this doubtful." · src:[S4][S1][S2] · purpose:period authority + a built-in doubt · beat:Hook · conf:high · role:context
3. Seven cities claimed Homer as their citizen; later Greek sources give only "not very useful scraps" about him. · src:[S4] · purpose:the man cannot be found · beat:Hook/Mirror · conf:high · role:challenges
4. Homer did not invent the story: he "had the story and many of the lines handed down to him by generations of oral poets" who sang of Troy and the wanderings. · src:[S4] · purpose:the tradition, not a single author, is the "life" behind the poem · beat:Mirror · conf:high · role:supports
5. In 1795 the German scholar F. A. Wolf argued the epics were not written by one man at one desk but grew from scattered oral songs, later edited into fixed form — the origin of "the Homeric Question." · src:[S3] · purpose:name the authorship doubt · beat:Mirror cluster · conf:high · role:challenges
6. Chinese "pseudo-history" (伪史论) voices push the doubt to an extreme: the epics are a *late forgery* because scholarship can't prove Homer existed. S3 presents this claim in order to **refute** it. · src:[S3] · purpose:the sanctioned SHOCK hook · beat:Hook · conf:high · role:challenges
7. Oral-formulaic fingerprints: fixed epithets — "rosy-fingered Dawn," "grey-eyed Athena," "resourceful Odysseus," "swift-footed Achilles" — are memory-and-rhythm machinery, not clumsiness. · src:[S4][S3] · purpose:proof of oral making · beat:Mirror cluster · conf:high · role:supports/challenges
8. Living proof of the method: oral singers as late as the early 20th c. (former Yugoslavia / the Balkans) improvised poems as long as the *Odyssey* with no written text. · src:[S4][S3] · purpose:the tradition is real, observable · beat:Mirror cluster · conf:high · role:supports
9. Inside the poem, a blind bard **Demodocus** rises at a feast and sings the fall of Troy — a self-portrait of the singer tucked into his own creation. · src:[S4][S1] · purpose:the poet mirrored inside the poem · beat:Mirror · conf:high · role:supports
10. The 24-book division matches the 24 letters of the Greek alphabet and was imposed ~400 years after composition, not by Homer. · src:[S4] · purpose:texture, "not a modern book" · beat:Mirror cluster · conf:high · role:context

## B. Archaeology & the real world behind the myth (context cluster)

11. Heinrich Schliemann — a businessman-turned-treasure-hunter obsessed with Homer since childhood — dug up a site at Hisarlik (NW Turkey) in the 1870s and claimed it as Troy / "Priam's treasure." · src:[S3] · purpose:myth had a real place · beat:Context · conf:high · role:challenges→supports
12. Schliemann was *led* to the site by the English diplomat Frank Calvert, who had already identified Hisarlik; Schliemann's huge trench (≈40m wide, 17m deep) badly damaged the site. · src:[S3] · purpose:honest correction, authority detail · beat:Context · conf:high · role:context
13. Linear B — Bronze-Age tablets found by Arthur Evans at Knossos, later at Pylos/Mycenae/Tiryns — was decoded by Michael Ventris in 1952 and shown to record an early Greek (Mycenaean Greek). · src:[S3] · purpose:writing DID exist behind the world the poem remembers · beat:Context · conf:high · role:challenges→supports
13b. The tablets are dry palace ledgers (grain, land, livestock, offerings, weapons) — "not the manuscript of the *Iliad*," but proof the Bronze-Age Greek world was not a blank, illiterate void. · src:[S3] · purpose:precise, honest scope · beat:Context · conf:high · role:context
14. Verdict S3 lands: the *Odyssey* sits "between myth, literature and remembered history" — not a history book, not a fairy tale, but memory reshaped by song. · src:[S3][S4] · purpose:the honest middle ground = thesis-safe · beat:Context/Thesis · conf:high · role:supports
15. Analogy S3 uses: version-complexity ≠ forgery — Chinese classics (*Dream of the Red Chamber*, its 脂/程高 recensions; Confucius "transmitted but did not create") have the same textual layering and no one calls them fakes. · src:[S3] · purpose:defuse the forgery charge in one stroke · beat:Hook/Context · conf:high · role:challenges

## C. The homecoming spine — stakes, home, and being known (SUPPORTS)

16. The Trojan War's events sit ~1200 BC; Odysseus is "most cunning of the Greeks," maker of the wooden horse that ended the war. · src:[S1][S2][S4] · purpose:who he is · beat:Thesis · conf:high · role:context
17. Odysseus never wanted the war: he feigned madness (plowing his fields with salt, yoking an ox and a donkey) to dodge the draft; Palamedes exposed him by laying baby Telemachus before the plough — Odysseus swerved, revealing his sanity and his love. · src:[S1] · purpose:home-vs-glory is his character from line one · beat:Thesis/Plot · conf:high · role:supports
18. "Throughout it all Odysseus only longed for one thing — to return to Ithaca and be reunited with Penelope and his son Telemachus." · src:[S2][S1] · purpose:the spine, stated plainly · beat:Thesis · conf:high · role:supports
19. The *Odyssey* focuses on "a husband trying to return home, a father desperate to see his son" — deliberately smaller than the *Iliad*'s grand battle. · src:[S2] · purpose:home is the subject, not war · beat:Thesis · conf:high · role:supports
20. The journey home took ten years and cost every man he led; the war plus the wandering = twenty years away. · src:[S1][S2] · purpose:the scale of the ache · beat:Plot · conf:high · role:supports

## D. Ithaca under siege — the home worth fighting for (SUPPORTS)

21. Over a hundred suitors, sure Odysseus is dead, occupy his hall, devour his herds and wine, and press Penelope to remarry. · src:[S1][S2] · purpose:the home is being eaten alive · beat:Plot · conf:high · role:supports
22. Penelope stalls three years by weaving a burial shroud for Odysseus's father Laertes and secretly unpicking it each night by lamplight — cunning that "nearly matched her husband's." · src:[S1][S2] · purpose:she is his equal; loyalty as active resistance · beat:Plot · conf:high · role:supports
23. Telemachus — an infant when his father sailed — keeps a vigil on the shore for twenty years, growing to a man in a house full of strangers eating his inheritance; Athena (as Mentor) rouses him to act. · src:[S1] · purpose:the son who never knew his father · beat:Plot · conf:high · role:supports

## E. The adventures reframed — the cost of needing to be known (SUPPORTS + one challenges-note)

24. The famous monsters — Cyclops, Circe, Sirens, Scylla/Charybdis — appear only in Books 9–12, and only inside the story Odysseus himself tells to King Alcinous; a different register, "quarantined" from the poem's realism. · src:[S4] · purpose:hidden-detail beat (folded in, not the spine) · beat:Adventures · conf:high · role:supports
25. In the Cyclops's cave Odysseus blinds Polyphemus and escapes by calling himself **"Nobody"** — so the other Cyclopes hear only that "Nobody" is hurting him. · src:[S1][S2] · purpose:survival by erasing the self · beat:Adventures · conf:high · role:supports
26. Then pride undoes him: he shouts his real name across the waves; Polyphemus (son of Poseidon) names him to his father, and Poseidon curses him to lose his crew and wander ten years. · src:[S1][S2] · purpose:the tragic mechanism — the need to be known costs him everything · beat:Adventures · conf:high · role:supports
27. Losses of the crew are self-inflicted foolishness he warned against: the bag of winds opened, the cattle of Helios eaten — "it's their fault." He endures; they don't. · src:[S1][S2][S4] · purpose:perseverance & self-control as the new heroism · beat:Adventures · conf:high · role:supports
28. Seven years held by the nymph Calypso, weeping on the shore every day "wishing to see his wife and son"; Athena persuades Zeus, Hermes frees him. · src:[S1][S2] · purpose:even paradise is exile if it isn't home · beat:Adventures · conf:high · role:supports

## F. The argument in one breath — home/survival over glory (SUPPORTS, the decelerate climax)

29. The poem repeatedly sets Odysseus beside **Agamemnon**: who came home from Troy fast, proud, in the open — and was murdered at his own table by an unfaithful wife (Clytemnestra) and her lover. Odysseus does the opposite: slow, secret, disguised, to a faithful wife. Penelope vs. Clytemnestra = patience vs. betrayal. · src:[S4][S1] · purpose:the master contrast device · beat:Climax · conf:high · role:supports
30. In the underworld the shade of **Achilles** — the greatest warrior, who chose a short glorious life — tells Odysseus he would rather "follow the plough as thrall to another man… than be a king over all the perished dead." · src:[S4] · purpose:the thesis stated by the *Iliad*'s own hero · beat:Climax · conf:high · role:supports
31. The *Iliad* worships glory; the *Odyssey* answers: *stay alive, come home, endure.* Its emblem is Odysseus clinging to a plank after shipwreck — greatness as stubborn survival, not the sword. · src:[S4] · purpose:the explicit reframe · beat:Climax · conf:high · role:supports
32. Guest-friendship (*xenia*) is a governing theme — how you treat a stranger; the poem is "an exemplary poem" about how people can live together. · src:[S4] · purpose:home = civilization, not just a house · beat:Climax · conf:med · role:supports

## G. The reunion — being known again (SUPPORTS, contemplative close)

33. Home in disguise as a beggar, Odysseus is recognized first by his old dog **Argos** (who dies of joy) and by his old nurse Eurycleia, who knows him by the boar-scar on his thigh as she washes his feet. · src:[S1][S4] · purpose:to be known by those who love you · beat:Close · conf:high · role:supports
34. Only Odysseus can string his great bow and shoot an arrow clean through twelve axe-heads; he then kills the suitors and reclaims his hall. · src:[S1][S2] · purpose:the homecoming earned · beat:Close · conf:high · role:supports
35. **The bed test:** Penelope tests him by ordering their bed moved; Odysseus erupts — the bed *cannot* be moved, he built it himself around a living olive tree still rooted in the earth. Only the true husband knows the secret. She runs to him. · src:[S1] · purpose:the rooted symbol of home — a marriage literally grown from the ground · beat:Close · conf:high · role:supports
36. The like-mindedness (*homophrosyne*) Odysseus names to Nausicaa — man and wife of one mind — is exactly what he and Penelope prove. · src:[S4] · purpose:the reunion's meaning · beat:Close · conf:med · role:supports

## H. Legacy & modern relevance (SUPPORTS, accelerate before the settle)

37. The quest/journey is a near-universal feature of epic (the *Aeneid*, *Paradise Lost*); the *Odyssey* is "the first and greatest story of a successful quest." · src:[S4] · purpose:scale/legacy · beat:Legacy · conf:high · role:context
38. Its shape runs from *Beowulf* to El Cid to "the modern-day road movie" — the epic quest as entertainment and self-discovery for every generation. · src:[S4] · purpose:modern-relevance jolt · beat:Legacy · conf:high · role:supports
39. S3's own modern hook: today's superhero stories (Iron Man, Batman, Spider-Man) — an ordinary person forged by ordeal into a hero — repeat the Homeric narrative frame. · src:[S3] · purpose:the "urgent now" parallel, drawn from the counter-source · beat:Legacy · conf:med · role:supports
40. The central message: the trials themselves — testing his character and courage — become "much more important than the reason for his trip or even his safe arrival home." · src:[S4] · purpose:the contemplative thesis to land on · beat:Close · conf:high · role:supports

### Source discrepancies noted (avoid pinning in narration)
- **Composition date:** ~700 BC (S4) vs. "2,800 years ago" (S1/S2). Keep loose ("nearly three thousand years ago").
- **Circe/son Telegonus** (S2) and some novelistic embellishments in S1 (Helen's Egyptian drug, feigned-madness scene) are single-source; used only as color, flagged in factcheck.
- **The proem line** ("Tell me of the man…" / "the man is the first word") — S4 states the first word is "man"; any English wording is paraphrase, no translator attributed.
