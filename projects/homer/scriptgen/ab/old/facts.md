# Fact Sheet — Homer / The Odyssey

**Through-line chosen:** *A poem about a man who survives by becoming "Nobody" — sung by a poet who is himself almost nobody at all.* The central contrast is **presence vs. absence**: the most famous homecoming in literature, written by an author history cannot locate. The "biographical mirroring" move is adapted to the Homeric Question — since Homer is unknowable, his absence becomes the subject, and the blind bard Demodocus inside the poem becomes the poet's self-portrait.

## Beat 1 — Hook: "Tell me of the man"

- The *Odyssey* opens with an invocation to the Muse; the first word of the poem is "man." `[S4]`
- Around 700 BC a blind poet known as Homer produced two works that became the foundation of Western literature: the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey*. `[S1] [S4]`
- The *Iliad* = the siege of Troy; the *Odyssey* = Odysseus's journey home. `[S1] [S2] [S4]`
- Odysseus escapes the Cyclops by calling himself "Nobody." `[S1] [S2]`
- The Greek word for the name he gives ("Outis"/Nobody wordplay). `[model: needs-check]`

## Beat 2 — The poet we cannot find (the "biography," adapted)

- We know almost nothing about Homer; later Greek sources give only a few not-very-useful scraps. `[S4]`
- Seven cities claimed Homer as their citizen. `[S4]`
- He was said to be blind, though the richness of his visual imagery makes this doubtful. `[S4]`
- In 1795 the German scholar Wolf (F. A. Wolf) published a study arguing the *Iliad*/*Odyssey* were not composed at once by one poet named Homer, but grew from an earlier oral tradition, later edited into fixed versions — this began the "Homeric Question." `[S3]`
- Latin title *Prolegomena ad Homerum*. `[model: needs-check]`
- The poems were sung, handed down across generations of oral poets, in an age on the border between oral and written culture. `[S3] [S4]`
- Inside the poem (Book 8), a blind singer named Demodocus sings the tale of Troy at a feast — an example of an oral poet embedded in the work. `[S4]`

## Beat 3 — The world that sang it (context)

- The poem uses fixed formulae / set phrases repeated whenever an idea recurs: e.g. "Dawn with her rosy fingers," "grey-eyed Athena," repeated lines. `[S4]`
- Fixed epithets such as "swift-footed Achilles," "resourceful/ much-devising Odysseus" are oral-composition tools that aid memory and rhythm, not flaws. `[S3]`
- The "Oral-Formulaic Theory" came from studying living oral singers; last century scholars found singers (in the former Yugoslavia / Balkans) still improvising poems as long as the *Iliad* or *Odyssey* with no written text, about events centuries earlier. `[S3] [S4]`
- The Troy of the poem stood around 1200 BC (the events the oral poets told of). `[S4]`
- Heinrich Schliemann — a successful businessman, not a trained archaeologist, obsessed with Homer since childhood, who learned ancient Greek and believed the *Iliad* preserved real historical memory — claimed to find Troy at Hisarlik in NW Turkey. `[S3]`
- The site was actually first identified by an English-descended diplomat, Frank Calvert, who persuaded Schliemann. `[S3]`
- Schliemann's trench (~40 m wide, ~17 m deep) cut through the center of the site and caused major damage; in 1873 he found gold/silver objects he claimed were "Priam's treasure"; the find shook Europe. `[S3]`
- Bronze-Age Greece had a script, Linear B: Arthur Evans excavated tablets at Knossos on Crete (early 20th c.); similar tablets found at Pylos, Mycenae, Tiryns; Michael Ventris deciphered Linear B in 1952, confirming it recorded an early (Mycenaean) Greek. Tablets are mostly palace administrative records (grain, land, livestock, offerings, craftsmen, weapons) — not the poems themselves, but proof that world had writing, palaces, administration. `[S3]`
- The Odyssey is not a modern history book, but not pure fairy tale either — it sits between myth, literature, and historical memory. `[S3]`

## Beat 4 — The premise / cast (a kingdom of wolves)

- Odysseus was gone 20 years total — ten at Troy, ten wandering. `[S1] [S2]`
- On Ithaca, over a hundred suitors, believing him dead, infest his palace, devour his herds and wine, and pressure his wife Penelope to remarry. `[S1] [S2]`
- Penelope stalls them by weaving a burial shroud for Odysseus's father Laertes, secretly unravelling it each night; the trick lasted three years before discovery. `[S1] [S2]`
- Their son Telemachus was a baby/infant when Odysseus left for Troy and has grown up fatherless. `[S1] [S2] [S4]`
- Odysseus's motive throughout is to return to wife, son, and home — not glory. `[S1] [S2] [S4]`

## Beat 5 — Nobody's story (the analytical heart: the monsters)

- The fantastic creatures — Cyclops, Sirens, Scylla — appear only in books 9–12 (about a sixth of the 24-book poem), and only inside the story Odysseus himself tells to the Phaeacians; a "different register" set against the realism of the rest. `[S4]`
- The Odyssey was divided into 24 books (matching the 24 letters of the Greek alphabet) about 400 years after composition — not Homer's own division. `[S4]`
- The poem can also be seen as six parts of four books each. `[S4]`
- In his own narration Odysseus appears less admirable — he is foolish to reveal his real name to the Cyclops, which provokes Poseidon's wrath. `[S4]`
- Cyclops Polyphemus is a son of Poseidon; Odysseus blinds him, escapes, then reveals his true name; Poseidon curses him to wander/lose his crew for ten years. `[S1] [S2]`
- Odysseus's key traits: perseverance (symbolized by clinging to a plank after shipwreck) and self-control, driven by wanting to get home. `[S4]`

## Beat 6 — The cost of coming home (parallels & theme)

- The poem repeatedly sets Odysseus beside Agamemnon: Agamemnon returned from Troy quickly, openly, as victor — and was murdered on his homecoming by his unfaithful wife Clytemnestra and her lover Aegisthus. `[S4]`
- Odysseus returns slowly, alone, by deception, and emerges victorious; he "learns from Agamemnon's mistakes." `[S4]`
- Penelope is held up in contrast to Clytemnestra (faithful vs. murderous). `[S4]`
- In the underworld, Achilles tells Odysseus he would rather be a living slave to a poor, landless master than be king over all the dead. `[S2] [S4]`
- The message of the *Odyssey* — that it is important to stay alive — differs from the *Iliad*, where heroism/glory matters most. `[S4]`
- Odysseus has one divine friend (Athena) and one divine enemy (Poseidon, for personal reasons — the blinding of his son). `[S4]`
- The ideal between man and wife is "like-mindedness," which Penelope and Odysseus demonstrate. `[S4]` (Greek term *homophrosyne*. `[model: needs-check]`)

## Beat 7 — Legacy & contemplative close

- The *Odyssey* is the first and greatest story of a successful quest; from Beowulf to El Cid to the modern road movie, the epic quest recurs for every generation. `[S4]`
- Homeric epic has been the absolute foundation of the Western literary edifice for 2,000+ years; modern hero-growth stories, including superhero films (Iron Man, Batman, Spider-Man), repeat the Homeric narrative framework. `[S3]`
- Homecoming details: Odysseus returns disguised as a beggar; the faithful old nurse (Eurycleia) recognizes him by his scar, and his old dog Argos recognizes him and dies. `[S4] [S1]` (dog named Argos, nurse named Eurycleia `[model: needs-check]`)
- The contest of the bow: Penelope challenges the suitors to string Odysseus's bow and shoot an arrow through twelve axe-heads; only the disguised Odysseus can do it; he then slays the suitors (Telemachus having hidden their weapons). `[S1] [S2]`
- Reunion of Odysseus and Penelope after 20 years. `[S1] [S2] [S4]`
