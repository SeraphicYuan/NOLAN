## The boom is real [1:41]

Now, let's start with the part people online really don't want to hear: this boom is real, and it is not stopping. The money is genuinely industrial-revolution money. Big tech is on track to spend more than $800 billion on AI infrastructure in 2026, and north of a trillion dollars in 2027 — that's basically the entire US defense budget, poured into server farms. And the demand underneath it is real too. OpenAI says its chatbot users doubled in a single year, from 400 to 900 million. That's why the power numbers are going vertical: data centers ate about 4.4% of all US electricity in 2023, and that could hit 7 to 12% by 2028. A single AI prompt uses roughly ten times the juice of a plain Google search. And here's the part the critics skip — some towns genuinely cash in. Out in Loudoun County, Virginia, data centers throw off about 38% of the entire county budget, enough that they actually cut property taxes. So no, this isn't all downside. We are not un-building this thing.

## So where does the bill land? [2:49]

But — and you knew there was a but coming — the benefits here are spread out across all of us, while the costs land on very specific people. So let's follow the bill. Start with your power. In Virginia, Dominion Energy says it has to roughly double how much electricity it generates by 2039, mostly to feed data centers, at a cost of up to $103 billion — and that could push residential bills up by as much as 50%. Then there's water. Up to 43% of the biggest data centers sit in areas of high water stress, and one planned Microsoft campus out in the Arizona desert was set to drink 1.83 billion gallons a year — enough for about 61,000 people, a city the size of Santa Cruz, California. Now picture this. You're in Virginia, and there's a low drone you don't just hear — you feel it in the walls. One guy, Carlos, spent twenty grand on new windows and insulation and still couldn't sleep, and his seven-year-old started having nightmares about a spaceship parked outside. In fairness, Amazon says its centers run well below the required sound levels. And in Georgia and Kentucky, some folks are losing their land outright — eminent domain, for the power lines. And the kicker? These places don't even get the jobs. Even the largest data centers run on fewer than 150 permanent workers once they're built — some as few as 25. OpenAI's giant Stargate site in Texas? About a hundred full-time jobs.

## And nobody really voted on it [4:24]

Now let's zoom out, because there's a thread running through all of that, and it's the part that honestly gets under my skin. Practically nobody agreed to any of this. There's no public list of where these things are or who owns them — reporters at Business Insider had to reverse-engineer a whole map out of backup-generator permits, because the companies redact everything as a trade secret. Ownership hides behind shell companies: Google bought land through a Delaware shell called "Jetstream," and Meta locked in a $60 million tax deal in Ohio under the name "Sicat" before anyone realized it was Facebook. This isn't even new — Disney pulled the exact same move in the sixties, buying up Florida through a shell literally named "M.T. Lot" — empty lot, get it — for a hundred bucks an acre. But here's the single most telling detail in this whole debate. That Stargate megasite in Texas? Its first approvals came through as a "permit by rule" — the same no-public-meeting fast-track you'd hand an auto-body shop or a dry cleaner. For a power plant that could light up a whole city. A former Texas regulator basically admitted the industry is now growing faster than the agency can even track it. So this isn't Luddites panicking about robots. It's a consent problem.

## Both sides, honestly [5:47]

Okay, so let me steel-man the other way now, because I said I would. A lot of the viral panic really is overblown. Nationally, all the water these data centers use adds up to about 5% of what US golf courses drink — so no, the country is not running dry. They don't cause blackouts; grids plan years ahead. Some designs use basically no water at all, and Meta even runs a site in Denmark that pipes its waste heat into people's homes. So that "the whole county gets wiped off the map" stuff? That's the overreach — and honestly, it just hands Big Tech an easy villain to point at. But the boosters are dodging too. Those "water positive by 2030" pledges mostly mean paying somebody else to save water, not using less on site. And the efficiency argument runs headfirst into a 160-year-old idea called the Jevons paradox: make something cheaper to run, and people just run way more of it. So who's right? Personally, I lean toward the folks getting handed the bill — but that's my preference, not my prediction. Practically speaking, they're both right, to some degree. The panic's wrong about the scale. The boosters are wrong about the consent and the cost.
