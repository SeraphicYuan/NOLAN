## The boom is real [2:12]

Now, let's start with the side people online don't want to hear, which is that this boom is real, and it's not stopping. The money is genuinely industrial-revolution money. Big tech is on track to spend more than $800 billion on AI infrastructure in 2026, and north of a trillion in 2027 — that's basically the entire US defense budget, on server farms. And the demand is real too: OpenAI says chatbot users doubled in a single year, from 400 to 900 million. That's why the power numbers are exploding — data centers ate about 4.4% of all US electricity in 2023, and that could hit 7 to 12% by 2028. A single AI prompt uses roughly ten times the power of a Google search. And here's the part critics skip: some towns genuinely cash in. In Loudoun County, Virginia, data centers throw off about 38% of the county's general budget — enough that they actually cut property taxes. So no, this isn't all downside. We are not un-building this thing.

## So where does the bill land? [3:25]

But — and you knew there was a but — here's the catch. The benefits are spread out across all of us, and the costs land on very specific people. So let's follow the bill. Start with your electricity. In Virginia, Dominion Energy says it has to roughly double how much power it generates by 2039, mostly for data centers, at a cost of up to $103 billion — and that could push residential bills up by as much as 50%. In some places it's worse than a bill. Out at Lake Tahoe, the Nevada utility told the local one it'll straight-up stop supplying power after May 2027, because it needs that capacity for data centers — leaving around 49,000 residents to find electricity somewhere else. Then there's water. Up to 43% of the biggest data centers sit in areas of high water stress, and one planned Microsoft campus in the Arizona desert was set to use 1.83 billion gallons a year — enough for about 61,000 people. And it's not just resources; it's your actual quiet. Picture this: you're in Virginia, and there's a low drone you can't just hear, you can feel it in the walls. In Georgia and Kentucky, some folks are losing their land outright to eminent domain for the power lines. And the kicker? These places don't even get the jobs. Even the largest data centers run on fewer than 150 permanent workers once they're built — some as few as 25. OpenAI's giant Stargate site in Texas? About a hundred full-time jobs.

## And nobody really voted on it [5:45]

Now let's zoom out, because there's a thread running through all of that, and it's the part that actually gets me. Practically nobody agreed to any of this. ## Both sides, honestly [6:55]

Okay, so let me steel-man the other way now, because I said I would. A lot of the viral panic really is overblown. Nationally, all the water these data centers use adds up to about 5% of what US golf courses drink — so no, the country is not running dry. They don't cause blackouts; grids plan years ahead. Some designs use basically no water at all. Meta even runs a site in Denmark that pipes its waste heat into people's homes. So the "whole county gets wiped off the map" stuff? That's the overreach, and honestly it just gives Big Tech an easy villain to point at. But the boosters are dodging too — those "water positive by 2030" pledges mostly lean on paying someone else to save water, not using less on site. And the efficiency argument runs into a 160-year-old idea called the Jevons paradox: make something cheaper to run, and people just use way more of it. So who's right? Practically speaking, they're both right, to some degree. The panic's wrong about the scale. The boosters are wrong about the consent and the cost.
