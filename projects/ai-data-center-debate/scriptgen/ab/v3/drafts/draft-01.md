## What is this thing, actually [1:30]

First, the basics. When people say something lives "in the cloud," there is no cloud. It's a building. A data center is basically a factory for computation — rows of servers in a warehouse. What changed is that since ChatGPT dropped in late 2022, these buildings started filling up with GPUs, the chips AI runs on, and GPUs are hungry. One ChatGPT query pulls roughly ten times the electricity of a Google search. In 2023, U.S. data centers ate about 4.4% of the country's electricity; the Department of Energy figures that could hit 7 to 12% by 2028.

And the money is staggering. Big tech is projected to spend over $800 billion on this in 2026 alone, and over a trillion in 2027 — roughly the entire U.S. defense budget. So, to be fair up front: this is a real revolution, and nobody's turning it off.

## The scale, at your address [2:45]

But here's where it gets personal. Business Insider had to build the map themselves, because there's no official public registry of these things. Their tally: 1,240 data centers built or approved by the end of 2024 — nearly four times the number in 2010, going up more than two a week. In Loudoun County, Virginia — they literally call it "data center alley" — as much as a third of the planet's internet traffic runs through the state, and the data centers there ate almost a quarter of Virginia's entire electricity supply in 2023.

## The bedroom next door [3:40]

Now, zoom all the way back down. Picture this. You've lived on the same street in Prince William County, Virginia for thirty years. That's Donna Gallant. Then the data centers start rising up around her, and the noise — this low, tonal hum — gets so bad she can't sleep in her own bedroom. She ends up downstairs in noise-cancelling headphones just to sleep. A few miles south in Manassas, Carlos Janis spent about $20,000 replacing his windows and insulation, and still moved his family to the basement to escape the vibration. His seven-year-old started having nightmares — told his dad there was "a spaceship outside." And in Abilene, Texas, a military family next to the massive Stargate project put it this way: "We serve our country, we invest in our home, and we weren't even included in this process. I feel trapped."

To be fair, Amazon says its centers operate well below the required sound levels, and that's probably true on paper. But the American Public Health Association will tell you chronic noise isn't nothing — it's linked to real cardiovascular stress. So "technically within limits" and "fine to live next to" are not quite the same sentence.

## Water, money, and who gets the bill [5:00]

And it's not just noise. Up to 43% of the largest data centers sit in areas of high water stress. Microsoft's Arizona plan penciled out to 1.83 billion gallons of water a year — a city the size of Santa Cruz — in the middle of a desert drought. Meanwhile, who pays for the power? In Virginia, Dominion Energy says it needs to roughly double its generation by 2039 — a build-out that could push residential electric bills up by as much as 50%.

So what do the towns get back? This is the part that gets me. Even the biggest centers employ fewer than 150 permanent people once running — some as few as 25. Stargate needed about 1,500 workers to build it and will run on roughly 100. A cheese-packing plant on the same footprint was projected to bring 500 jobs. And the tax breaks are enormous — Virginia handed out almost a billion dollars in a single year. In Ohio, a town gave "Sidecat LLC" a 100% property-tax break for fifteen years, then found out Sidecat was Meta — one of several shell-company deals Business Insider traced back to big tech. And sometimes it goes further: to run new transmission lines, energy companies are invoking eminent domain and taking people's land outright. One woman in Georgia is watching her childhood home get taken for exactly that.

## The honest weighing [6:30]

Now let me steel-man the other side, because fairness demands it. Do data centers cause blackouts? No — grids plan years ahead. Are the diesel generators running 24/7? No, they're emergency backup. Are they all water-guzzlers? Also no — some run fully closed-loop and use almost none. Nationally, all data-center water use adds up to about 5% of what U.S. golf courses use. And plenty of towns genuinely cash in — Loudoun County says data centers throw off about 38% of its general-fund revenue, enough that it actually cut property taxes. We've panicked about new tech before, too; people once swore power lines would electrocute the neighborhood.

So they're probably both right, to some degree. But here's the honest catch. There's this thing called the Jevons Paradox: back in the 1860s, an economist noticed that making coal use more efficient didn't shrink demand — it grew it. AI chips are getting more efficient too. It almost certainly won't be enough. And some builders aren't even waiting for the rules — in Mississippi, xAI parked gas turbines on trailers and called them "temporary" to skip permits, which one former Texas regulator says her agency simply can't keep up with anymore.

So where does that leave us? Honestly, the question was never "AI or no AI." That ship has sailed; the tech is here, and a lot of it is genuinely useful. The real problem is quieter.
