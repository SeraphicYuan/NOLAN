## Hook [0:00 - 0:56]

Look, if you thought you'd be able to sit this whole AI revolution out — that it was a Silicon Valley problem, a thing that happens to other people — I need to point you toward Lake Tahoe. This is one of the wealthiest corners of California. Median home values north of $700,000. And right now, roughly 50,000 people who live there have no idea where their electricity is coming from after the spring of 2027. Because the Nevada utility that's powered the region for decades told the little California company that serves it, essentially, "we're going to stop sending you power" — it needs that capacity for data centers. That's Fortune's reporting. One resident put it about as plainly as you can: "It's like we don't exist." So today, we need to talk about the AI data center debate.

## Ground Rules [0:56 - 1:52]

Now, here's the thing. Before we get into it, let me set a couple of ground rules, because this topic makes people lose their minds in both directions. On one side, you've got folks at city council meetings screaming that the water's going to stop and the whole county is finished. On the other, you've got trillion-dollar companies putting up warehouses the size of small cities and hiding who owns them. I'm not going to do the hysteria thing — honestly, it's the fastest way to discredit a real concern, and there are real concerns here. So the honest question isn't "is AI good or evil." It's simpler: is there a real problem, who's paying for it, and are they the same people getting the benefit? Let's get into it. (And if this is useful — like and subscribe. You know the drill.)

## The Scale [1:52 - 2:47]

First, the scale, because you can't argue about something you can't see. Business Insider spent months filing public-records requests just to build a map, because there's no official directory and no single regulator to ask. What they found: at least 1,240 data centers built or approved in the US by the end of 2024. That's nearly four times as many as in 2010, and new ones are going up at more than two a week. A handful of hyperscalers — Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and Meta — own roughly a third of all the capacity. And the money is genuinely hard to picture. In 2025 alone, Meta budgeted around $64 billion, Google $75 billion, Microsoft $80 billion. The Stargate project in Texas — a single site — sprawls across about 1,100 acres. That's bigger than Central Park, for one complex.

## The Power Bill [2:47 - 3:58]

So what does all that actually cost? Start with power, because that's the real bottleneck. A Department of Energy report estimated data centers could soon eat up to 12% of all US electricity — in 2023, it was just over 4%. To put one facility in human terms: the IEA says a single large data center can use as much electricity as 400,000 electric cars. Now, companies almost always announce these projects promising renewables or nuclear. But a lot of what's actually getting built right now is natural gas — and in some places, coal that was supposed to be retired. In Nebraska, a utility voted to postpone shutting two coal plants and then approved two new gas plants, largely to feed data-center demand. And the tab doesn't always land on the tech company. In Virginia, Dominion Energy said it would need to roughly double its generation by 2039, at a cost of up to $103 billion — and warned residential electricity bills could climb by as much as 50%. That's not the data center paying. That's your grandmother's power bill.

## Water & the Neighbors [3:58 - 5:14]

Now let's zoom in, from the grid down to the guy next door. Business Insider found that up to 43% of the largest data centers sit in areas of high or extremely high water stress — and they use drinking water to cool the servers. Documents showed Microsoft planned for buildings in Arizona to use around a million gallons a day — roughly 1.8 billion gallons a year, about what a city the size of Santa Cruz uses. Then there's the noise. In Northern Virginia, a man named Carlos spent about $20,000 on new windows and insulation to escape the constant hum from three Amazon data centers a couple hundred meters away. His seven-year-old started having nightmares. Amazon says the centers run well below required sound levels — and that's probably true, which is sort of the problem. The rules were never written for a 24/7 industrial drone next to a subdivision. And a separate ABC News investigation flew thermal drones over Texas data centers and found real pollution plumes — fine particles linked to asthma and heart problems — drifting toward homes whose owners were never told the plant was coming.

## The Jobs Myth [5:14 - 6:27]

Okay, but surely the towns get something back — jobs, tax money? This is where the pitch gets shaky. The usual deal is: we hand a corporation a giant tax break, and they bring jobs. Except data centers barely have jobs. Business Insider found even the largest ones employ fewer than 150 permanent workers — some as few as 25. The Wall Street Journal reported that Stargate in Abilene, Texas will run on about 100 full-time employees. A cheese-packaging plant on the same-size footprint was projected to employ 500. Meanwhile the incentives are enormous — 37 states offer them, and in Virginia alone, 56 projects racked up nearly a billion dollars in tax savings in a single year. And in the worst cases, people aren't just missing out — they're losing ground. Rents in one Louisiana parish jumped from around $650 to $2,000 a month once Meta showed up. In Georgia and Kentucky, some homeowners and farmers say they've been threatened with eminent domain to clear a path for the power lines. So the benefit tends to flow up, and a lot of the cost flows down.

## Both Sides [6:27 - 7:38]

Now, I promised I'd be fair, so let me steel-man the other side — it isn't all grim. Sited well, these things can genuinely pay off. In Loudoun County, Virginia, the world's biggest data-center hub, the county says these facilities generate about 38% of its general-fund revenue — enough that they've actually cut property taxes. That's real money for schools and roads. And the water doom isn't universal either: the newest closed-loop cooling systems can push water use close to zero, and nationally, data-center water use is a small fraction of what golf courses consume. So the "every data center drains a reservoir" line just isn't accurate. Here's my honest read, though. The technology keeps getting more efficient — but there's a 160-year-old idea called the Jevons paradox that says efficiency doesn't shrink demand, it grows it. Cheaper compute means more compute. So both sides are kind of right at once: individually, these things are getting cleaner; collectively, they're still exploding. The problem was never really the machine. It's the speed, the secrecy, and who gets handed the bill.

## Close [7:38 - 8:28]

So where does that leave us? Not "ban the data centers." AI isn't going back in the box, and some of this buildout genuinely is the next industrial revolution. But racing forward with no map, no public vote, and shell companies quietly buying up the neighborhood — that's a choice, and we're making it by default. The reasonable ask here isn't hysteria. It's power-first planning, real transparency on water, and treating the communities that host these things as partners instead of collateral. That's all I've got for today.
