## Sung, not written [0:00]

Because that is what these poems were — not books, but songs. The story, and many of its very lines, were handed to Homer by generations of oral poets who had sung them for centuries before anyone wrote them down. And we know such singers were real: as late as the early twentieth century, scholars in the mountains of the former Yugoslavia found bards reciting poems as long as the Odyssey itself, from memory, without a single written note.

Which quietly rescues something readers have complained about for ages. Homer repeats himself — "swift-footed Achilles," "gray-eyed Athena," Dawn arriving again and again "with her rosy fingers," the sea forever "wine-dark." It can read like a poet running low on ideas. It is the exact opposite. Those fixed phrases are the machinery of memory — the handholds a singer grips to raise a vast poem in real time, in front of a living crowd. Even the shape we know is an afterthought: the Odyssey's twenty-four books, one for each letter of the Greek alphabet, were carved up some four hundred years after it was composed. The poem was a breath long before it was a text. And a breath has no single author. It belongs to everyone who ever drew it.

## The wanderer earns his myth [1:23]

Now watch what this vanished, sightless poet chose to make his hero. Not the strongest man at Troy — the cleverest. This is a man who, called to war, feigned madness and ploughed his fields with salt — until the heralds laid his infant son in the plough's path, and he swerved, and gave himself away. In a world that worshipped Achilles and brute glory, Homer built his second epic around a hero who wins by his wits.

Trapped in the cave of the Cyclops Polyphemus — a son of the sea-god Poseidon — Odysseus does not fight. He thinks. He gives his name as "Nobody," blinds the giant with a sharpened stake, and rides to freedom clinging beneath a ram. Later, to hear the Sirens and live, he has his crew seal their ears with wax and lash him to the mast. Notice the pattern the blind poet keeps drawing: his hero triumphs not by looking but by SEEING — by inner sight, by a cunning so sharp that the help of Athena, goddess of wisdom, becomes impossible to tell apart from Odysseus's own racing mind. And notice his one great failure. Sailing away, drunk on victory, he shouts his real name back at the blinded Cyclops — and that single boast lets Polyphemus name him to his father, and buys Odysseus ten more years lost at sea.
