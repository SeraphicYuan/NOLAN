## Beat 1 — Hook / Reveal-seed (the Ploughman)
- Toward the **ploughman (laboureur)**, Death "helps … to plough his field" — an assistant, not an antagonist. [S2]
- Contrast: Death "kidnaps a child" cruelly; "skewers a knight"; "boldly pulls an abbot." [S2, S3]
- This gentleness toward the poor vs. harshness toward the powerful is the tonal break the whole series turns on. [S2, S3]

## Beat 2 — Establish the work
- Artist: **Hans Holbein the Younger (1497–1543)**. [S1]
- Designs cut **c. 1526, in Basel**. [S1] (S2: "circa 1526"; S3: "1523–25, Holbein in his twenties" — DATE SPREAD, treat as "mid-1520s".)
- Block-cutter: **Hans Lützelburger**, whose work was praised as "technically the most marvelous woodcuts ever made" (Ivins, quoting W. J. Linton). [S1]
- First book edition: **Les Simulachres et Historiées Faces de la Mort**, published at **Lyons by the Trechsel brothers in 1538**, containing **41 woodcuts**. [S1] (S2's page says "Basle" for 1538 — DISCREPANCY; prefer Lyons per S1.)
- The images are **woodcuts / prints**, a mass-reproducible form (not a single painting). [S1, S2]

## Beat 3 — Surface analysis (how to look)
- Structure: opens with **four Genesis scenes and skeletal musicians**; then **34 victims** in descending social hierarchy — pope, emperor, king, cardinal, empress, queen, bishop, duke, abbot, abbess, nobleman, canon, judge, advocate, senator, preacher, priest, monk, nun, old woman, physician, astrologer, rich man, merchant, seaman, knight, count, old man, countess, noblewoman, duchess, peddler, **ploughman**, and child; concludes with **Last Judgment and Death's escutcheon (coat of arms)**. [S2]
- Recurring prop: the **hourglass appears 25 of 35 times**. [S2]
- Recurring motif: **broken crowns and swords signify failed authority**. [S2]
- Each print is small — a book-page woodcut, not a wall-sized work. [S1, S2 — inferred from "woodcuts"/"book edition"; sizes not stated: model: needs-check for any exact dimension]

## Beat 4 — Context (biography / world)
- Made during Holbein's **Basel** years. [S1, S3]
- Basel was a hub of humanist printing and reform-era religious ferment. [model: needs-check]
- Holbein moved in humanist circles (e.g., Erasmus) in Basel. [model: needs-check]
- The series was created on the eve of / amid the early Reformation (Luther's protest began 1517). [model: needs-check]
- Holbein "refashioned the late-medieval allegory of the Danse Macabre as a **reformist satire**," reflecting a gradual shift toward reformed Christianity. [S1]

## Beat 5 — The Reveal (Ploughman pivot, expanded)
- Death's **dual character**: gentle helper to the ploughman; cruel abductor of the child; "solemnly attends" the elderly to their rest; to nobility Death is "invincible"; the rich man is robbed of "money before stealing his soul." [S2]
- The tonal break in the Ploughman cut recontextualizes the series: Death is not only terror, but sorting mercy from punishment by social station. [S2, S3]

## Beat 6 — Competing interpretations
- **Theory A (pointed satire):** the series is "social criticism of the wealthy and the Catholic church" — Death as kindness for the poor vs. horrific punishment for the corrupt powerful. [S3] (Note: S3 flags this as the *author's* reading, NOT a documented Holbein statement.)
- Supporting: "**Death is turned into a dispenser of justice, denouncing greediness and the abuse of power.**" [S2]
- **Theory B (the qualifier):** "**neither the preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms**" — because of their sympathy with the poor. A blanket anticlerical reading overshoots. [S2]

## Beat 7 — Recontextualization
- The tradition's core meaning: "the apparent class distinction … is completely **neutralized by Death as the ultimate equalizer**." [S1]
- Memento mori: Death confronts individuals of all stations; "**none escape Death's skeletal clutches, not even the pious**." [S1]
- So the series is at once (a) universal leveler and (b) a smuggled moral verdict on who deserves mercy. [S1, S2, S3]

## Beat 8 — Close (afterlife / scale)
- Popularity: "**eleven editions before 1562**, and over the sixteenth century perhaps as many as a **hundred unauthorized editions and imitations**." [S1]
- Expanded editions: **1545 (Lyon, +8 figures** by an unknown artist); **1562 (Lyon, 57 woodcuts** total). [S2]
