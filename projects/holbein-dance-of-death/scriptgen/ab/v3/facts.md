## Beat: ESTABLISH THE WORK
- Full title: *Les Simulachres et Historiées Faces de la Mort* ("images and illustrated faces of Death"), known as *The Dance of Death*. — src:[S1,S2] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- Designed by Hans Holbein the Younger (1497–1543) around **1526**, while living in **Basel**. — src:[S1] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- (Discrepancy) S3 dates the making to Holbein's Basel period **1523–25**, in his twenties. — src:[S3] · purpose:context · beat:context · conf:disputed · role:context
- Block-cutter: **Hans Lützelburger**, whose work was praised as "technically the most marvelous woodcuts ever made" (Ivins, quoting W. J. Linton). — src:[S1] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- First book edition: *Les Simulachres…*, published at **Lyons by the Trechsel brothers in 1538**, containing **41 woodcuts**. — src:[S1] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- (Discrepancy) S2 gives publication 1538 but prints "Basle" as the place; also lists 41 woodcuts. S1 (Lyons) is followed in the draft; discrepancy noted. — src:[S2] · purpose:context · beat:context · conf:disputed · role:context
- Immense popularity: "eleven editions before 1562," and perhaps "as many as a hundred unauthorized editions and imitations" over the 16th century. — src:[S1] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Expanded editions: 1545 (Lyon, +8 figures by an unknown artist); 1562 (Lyon, 57 woodcuts total). — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- Framed genre: memento mori — "remember you must die." — src:[S1] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports

## Beat: SURFACE ANALYSIS (how to look)
- Structure: opens with four Genesis scenes and skeletal musicians; concludes with the Last Judgment and Death's escutcheon (coat of arms). — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- The social ladder, top-down: pope, emperor, king, cardinal, empress, queen, bishop, duke, abbot, abbess, nobleman, canon, judge, advocate, senator, preacher, priest, monk, nun, old woman, physician, astrologer, rich man, merchant, seaman, knight, count, old man, countess, noblewoman, duchess, peddler, **ploughman**, and child — **34 victims**. — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- S1 counts 41 woodcuts total (Genesis + ranks + Last Judgment + escutcheon); S3 counts 34 individuals — consistent once framing scenes are separated from the ranks. — src:[S1,S3] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:context
- Recurring symbol: the **hourglass appears 25 of 35 times**, marking time running out. — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Broken crowns and swords signify failed authority. — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports

## Beat: CONTEXT (Reformation-era Basel)
- Holbein "refashioned the late-medieval allegory of the Danse Macabre as a **reformist satire**," reflecting a gradual shift toward reformed Christianity. — src:[S1] · purpose:authority · beat:context · conf:verified · role:supports
- The work is read as Reformation-era humanism / criticism of clerical excess. — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports

## Beat: THE REVEAL (the Ploughman) + differential treatment
- Toward the ploughman, Death "helps him to plough his field" (assistant, not antagonist). — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- Death's dual character across scenes: "kidnaps a child" cruelly; "solemnly attends" the elderly to their rest; nobility faces Death as "invincible"; the rich man is robbed of "money before stealing his soul." — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- Death "skewers a knight" and "boldly pulls an abbot," but "takes a gentler route when dealing with the less fortunate." — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:claimed · role:supports
- (Interpretive, author's reading, not a documented artist statement) The differential treatment demonstrates "social criticism of the wealthy and the Catholic church": kindness for the poor vs. horrific punishment for the corrupt powerful. — src:[S3] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:claimed · role:supports

## Beat: COMPETING INTERPRETATIONS / RECONTEXTUALIZATION
- Theory A (satire): "Death is turned into a dispenser of justice, denouncing greediness and the abuse of power." — src:[S2] · purpose:authority · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- Theory B / complication (universalist + anti-blanket-anticlerical): "neither the preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms" (their sympathy with the poor). — src:[S2] · purpose:contrast · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:challenges
- Theory B support: the leveler reading — class distinction neutralized, "none escape… not even the pious." — src:[S1] · purpose:contrast · beat:close · conf:verified · role:challenges
