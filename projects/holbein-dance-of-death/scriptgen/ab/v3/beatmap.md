# Beat-map — Holbein, *The Dance of Death*

Angle (spine): **Death treats a peasant more kindly than a pope — that single broken pattern is the
series' secret.** Guide = `art-stories/style_guide.md` (CONSTITUTION). Retention curve follows the guide's
tempo map: slow-to-seed → accelerate through exposition → **slowest at the reveal** → reflective close.
Braided, not chaptered. No numbered signposting, no isolated analysis beats.

## Hook · pace:decelerate · covers:[S1,S2] · serves-spine:
Guide HOOK type = **Paradox of Simplicity + Expert Blind Spot hybrid**. "We think we know what Death looks
like" — the merciless skeleton dragging popes and emperors — and the series shows exactly that, 40 times.
Promise: one of these 41 scenes breaks the pattern and rewrites the whole thing. Withhold *which* one.
(Pays off the guide's rule: one detail changes how you see the whole work.)

## Establish the work · pace:accelerate · covers:[S1,S2] · serves-spine:
Title *Les Simulachres… / The Dance of Death*; Holbein the Younger, ~1526, Basel; cut by Lützelburger;
first printed in Lyons, 1538; 41 woodcuts. Naïve first reading: "a grim medieval sermon in pictures" —
memento mori — the reading we'll overturn.

## Surface analysis (teach how to look) · pace:accelerate · covers:[S2] · serves-spine:
Each cut = one collision: a person mid-life + a skeleton arriving unannounced. The top-down social ladder
(pope → emperor → … → ploughman → child). Visual deixis on recurring props: the hourglass (25 of ~35),
broken crowns and snapped swords. Seeds the *pattern* the reveal will break.

## Context (Reformation Basel) · pace:accelerate · covers:[S1,S2] · serves-spine:
Basel on the eve of the Reformation; Holbein "refashioned the danse macabre as a reformist satire." Earn
biography AFTER the visual hook (guide DON'T: no biography dump first). Sets stakes for reading Death as a
judge of conduct, not just a reaper.

## Curiosity cliff-hanger → THE REVEAL (Ploughman) · pace:decelerate (slowest) · covers:[S2,S3] · serves-spine:
Tee-up: "in almost every scene Death takes something — the crown, the sword, the child. But turn to one
plate near the bottom of the ladder…" Reveal: with the **ploughman**, Death does not attack — it *helps him
drive his plough*. Slow down hard; let it sit. This is the emotional center and the promised single detail.

## Differential treatment as accelerated fact-cluster · pace:accelerate · covers:[S2,S3] · serves-spine:
Cash the pattern out against the reveal: rich man robbed of his money "before his soul," nobility faces an
"invincible" Death, the abbot boldly pulled, the child cruelly seized — versus the elderly "solemnly
attended" to rest and the ploughman helped. Contrast engine. (Controversy delivered as a fact-cluster, NOT
a lecture.)

## Competing interpretations · pace:decelerate · covers:[S2,S3,S1] · serves-spine:
Theory A (named reading): Death as "a dispenser of justice, denouncing greediness and the abuse of power" —
anticlerical/social satire (S2/S3). Then complicate it (guide's red-herring/debunk move): S2 — "neither the
preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms," and S1's leveler reading — "none escape, not even the
pious." So is the Ploughman protest, or mercy? Steelman both.

## Recontextualization · pace:decelerate · covers:[S1,S2] · serves-spine:
Re-label the series: not a flat memento mori and not simple anticlerical propaganda, but a moral ledger where
Death's *manner* is the message — the same figure is executioner to the corrupt and deliverer to the worn-out
poor. The Ploughman is the key that unlocks it.

## Reflective close + open question + sign-off · pace:decelerate · covers:[S1] · serves-spine:
Guide closing formula, in order: reflective reframe that KEEPS the mystery open (the leveler who is not quite
level; a hundred imitations couldn't settle what he meant) → open question to the viewer (protest or mercy?)
→ warm sign-off. No neat answer — ambiguity is the payoff.
