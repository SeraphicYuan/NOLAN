# Angles — Holbein, *The Dance of Death*

## Inferred spine type (from `art-stories/style_guide.md`)
**A single-artwork detective MYSTERY built on contrast, resolved into embraced ambiguity.** The guide's
spine is NOT biography, NOT argument, NOT "how it works." It is: *take one canonical work, claim it hides
a secret, teach the viewer how to look, then reveal ONE overlooked detail that recontextualizes the whole
thing — and refuse a tidy answer.* Its master arc is Mystery → Layered Reveal → Recontextualization →
Embraced Ambiguity; its engine is contrast/juxtaposition; its close is an open question, not a thesis.
The adaptation here: treat the 41-woodcut *series* as "the one work," and the **Ploughman** as its single
pivotal hidden detail — the guide's Act-3 reveal.

Every candidate below is of that type (a felt mystery-through-line about the series, paid off by one detail),
not a topic or a meta-question.

## Why Angle 1 wins (resonance + style-fit)
It IS the guide's spine in its purest form: it opens on a mystery the viewer feels in their gut (why is Death
*kind* to the peasant?), teaches how to look at the series, and pays off with **one pivotal detail — the
Ploughman** — exactly where the guide wants its Act-3 reveal. The Reformation-satire material (Angle 3) and
the "great leveler" cliché (Angle 2) are not discarded — they become the *grounding* and the *competing
interpretations* braided around the reveal, and the anticlerical read is deliberately complicated (S2:
"neither the preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms") so the script steelmans instead of
cheerleading, then lands on the guide's embraced ambiguity: is the Ploughman's gentleness protest, or mercy?
