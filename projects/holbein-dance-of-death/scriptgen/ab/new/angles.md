# Candidate Angles — Holbein, *The Dance of Death*

Thesis = a debatable claim, not a summary. The brief supplies a starting angle ("Death as the great leveler + smuggled anticlerical satire") and a hidden-detail pivot (the Ploughman). Both are folded in below as candidate material, but the choice is mine (auto mode).

---

**Angle 1 — The series only pretends to be about death; it is really a portable weapon of the Reformation, smuggling anticlerical satire past the censor as piety.**
- *supported by:* [S1] "reformist satire," "reformed Christianity"; [S2] "dispenser of justice, denouncing… abuse of power"; [S1] ~100 unauthorized editions (it spread like propaganda)
- *tension:* [S2] states plainly that "neither the preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms" — a purely anticlerical reading overreaches.
- *why compelling:* reframes a devotional keepsake as covert political media on the eve of a schism.

**Angle 2 — Death as the great leveler: the real radicalism is not who Death kills but that rank buys no exemption.**
- *supported by:* [S1] "Death as the ultimate equalizer," "none escape… not even the pious"; [S2] 34 victims ranked pope-to-ploughman; broken crowns and swords
- *tension:* if everyone dies equally, where is the "satire"? Leveling alone is the old medieval message, not new.
- *why compelling:* the stakes are social — a hierarchy-obsessed age handed an image where the pope and the peddler share one fate.

**Angle 3 — [CHOSEN] Holbein's Death is not a terror but a moral auditor, and the gentle Ploughman is the hidden detail that proves the series attacks *power*, not *the poor* — and not simply *the clergy*.**
- *supported by:* [S2] Death "helps" the ploughman plough his field vs. "kidnaps a child," robs the rich man, is "invincible" to nobility; [S2] "dispenser of justice… abuse of power"; [S3] "gentler route… for the less fortunate"; [S1] "ultimate equalizer"
- *tension:* [S2] "neither the preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms" — so it is anti-*abuse-of-power*, not flatly anticlerical; the script must hold that line honestly.
- *why compelling:* the one tonal break (Death as helper, not hunter) recontextualizes all 34 cuts — the leveler has a conscience, and its mercy is aimed downward, its cruelty upward.

**Angle 4 — The unsung co-author: Lützelburger's knife, not just Holbein's pen, made these the "most marvelous woodcuts ever made" — and the medium (cheap, reproducible print) is what turned a moral into a movement.**
- *supported by:* [S1] Lützelburger credit + Ivins/Linton praise; [S1] 11 editions before 1562, ~100 imitations; [S2] editions of 1538/1545/1562
- *tension:* thins the moral/political argument into a craft-and-media story; risks under-serving the artwork's meaning.
- *why compelling:* a hidden-collaborator reframe — the print run, not the pulpit, is why we still see these.

**Angle 5 — A memento mori that flinches: the series' cruelty to a child and tenderness to a labourer betray Holbein's own hand of judgment, breaking the genre's supposed neutrality.**
- *supported by:* [S2] child "kidnapped" cruelly, elderly "solemnly attended," ploughman helped; [S3] gentler to the less fortunate
- *tension:* [S1] frames the tradition as *neutralizing* class — so is Holbein neutral or taking sides? Sources pull both ways.
- *why compelling:* the contrast device is built in; the "neutral" allegory turns out to have a temperature.

---

**Chosen: Angle 3.** It wins on evidence and resonance: it is the only thesis carried by the pivotal Ploughman detail *and* forced to survive the strongest counter-fact in the sources ([S2]'s "neither preacher nor priest"), so it can steelman rather than cheerlead. It absorbs the brief's leveler + satire framing while sharpening it into a debatable claim — Death as moral auditor aimed at power — and pays off the hidden-detail promise the style guide demands.
