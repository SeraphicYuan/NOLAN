## Beat: evidence — reach, structure, symbol system
- There were eleven editions before 1562, and perhaps ~100 unauthorized editions/imitations across the 16th century — src:[S1] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Expanded editions followed: 1545 (Lyon, +8 figures by an unknown artist); 1562 (Lyon, 57 woodcuts total) — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- The series opens with four Genesis scenes and skeletal musicians, and concludes with the Last Judgment and Death's escutcheon (coat of arms) — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- 34 victims are shown, arranged in social hierarchy: pope, emperor, king, cardinal, empress, queen, bishop, duke, abbot, abbess, nobleman, canon, judge, advocate, senator, preacher, priest, monk, nun, old woman, physician, astrologer, rich man, merchant, seaman, knight, count, old man, countess, noblewoman, duchess, peddler, ploughman, and child — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- The count of "34 individuals" is corroborated by a second source — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- The hourglass appears 25 of 35 times across the images — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports
- Broken crowns and swords signify failed/collapsed authority — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:evidence · conf:verified · role:supports

## Beat: hook / turn — the argument (leveler + satire)
- The tradition's meaning: class distinction is "completely neutralized by Death as the ultimate equalizer" — src:[S1] · purpose:hook · beat:hook · conf:verified · role:supports
- Memento mori: Death confronts individuals of all stations; "none escape Death's skeletal clutches, not even the pious" — src:[S1] · purpose:proof · beat:hook · conf:verified · role:supports
- Holbein refashioned the late-medieval Danse Macabre allegory as a "reformist satire," reflecting a gradual shift toward reformed Christianity — src:[S1] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- "Death is turned into a dispenser of justice, denouncing greediness and the abuse of power" — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- **CHALLENGES the blanket anticlerical read:** "neither the preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms" (their sympathy with the poor) — src:[S2] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:challenges
- The rich man is robbed of "money before stealing his soul"; nobility faces Death as "invincible" — src:[S2] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports

## Beat: turn / close — the Ploughman pivot (Death's dual character)
- Toward the ploughman (laboureur), Death "helps … to plough his field" — an assistant, not an antagonist — src:[S2] · purpose:hook · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- By contrast, Death "kidnaps a child" cruelly and "solemnly attends" the elderly to their rest — src:[S2] · purpose:contrast · beat:turn · conf:verified · role:supports
- A second reading: Death "skewers a knight" and "boldly pulls an abbot" but "takes a gentler route when dealing with the less fortunate" — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:claimed · role:supports
- Interpretive claim (author's reading, not a documented artist statement): the gentleness-to-poor / harshness-to-powerful contrast shows "social criticism of the wealthy and the Catholic church" — src:[S3] · purpose:proof · beat:turn · conf:claimed · role:supports
