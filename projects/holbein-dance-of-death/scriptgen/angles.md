**[CHOSEN]** — *Death as the great leveler — and a smuggled social/anticlerical satire on the eve of the Reformation.* (SUPPLIED by the human; adopted.)

Why it fits the spine type:
- It IS a central contrast — the very juxtaposition the guide calls "the engine of the whole style": the medieval promise that Death flattens all ranks, set against Holbein's smuggled verdict that Death flatters some ranks and humiliates others.
- It has a genuine **hidden detail** that recontextualizes everything: the **Ploughman cut**, where Death stops being an executioner and becomes a helper at the plough. That is the "one detail changes how you read the whole work" the hook must promise and Act 3 must pay off.
- It carries **competing interpretations** with named sources: the traditional *memento mori* / "great equalizer" reading (S1) vs. the "reformist satire" / social-critique reading (S1, S3) — and a built-in **steelman** that keeps ambiguity honest: "neither the preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms" (S2), which complicates any blanket anticlerical claim.
- Resonant + style-fit: universal mortality is the emotional back-third the guide wants; the satire is the intellectual mystery.

Handling the supplied "anticlerical" word: honored, but **not overstated** — the script frames it as one theory and immediately complicates it with the preacher/priest counter-evidence, per the guide's Do ("present at least two interpretations") and its non-resolution rule.
