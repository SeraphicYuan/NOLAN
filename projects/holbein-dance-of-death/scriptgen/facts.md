## The work & its making
- Designed by **Hans Holbein the Younger (1497–1543)** while living in **Basel**, roughly **1523–1526**. `[S1]` (1526), `[S3]` (1523–25)
- Cut in wood by **Hans Lützelburger**, a master block-cutter; his work called "technically the most marvelous woodcuts ever made." `[S1]`
- Hans Lützelburger **died in 1526, before the series was published**. `[model: needs-check]` — NOT stated in any fetched source; widely reported elsewhere but unverified here → soften or attribute, do not state flatly.
- First book edition: ***Les Simulachres et Historiées Faces de la Mort***, **41 woodcuts**, published in **Lyon by the Trechsel brothers, 1538**. `[S1]`
  - DISCREPANCY: `[S2]` says published in "Basle" 1538. Wikipedia `[S1]` (Lyon, Trechsel brothers) is the more specific/authoritative → use Lyon; note the conflict in factcheck.
- Hugely popular: **eleven editions before 1562**, perhaps **~100 unauthorized editions/imitations** across the 16th c. `[S1]`
- Expanded later editions: 1545 (Lyon, +8 figures); 1562 (Lyon, **57 woodcuts** total). `[S2]`

## Structure & subjects
- Opens with **four Genesis scenes + skeletal musicians**; closes with the **Last Judgment** and **Death's escutcheon/coat of arms**. `[S2]`
- **34 social-rank "victims"** in descending hierarchy: pope, emperor, king, cardinal, empress, queen, bishop, duke, abbot, abbess, nobleman, canon, judge, advocate, senator, preacher, priest, monk, nun, physician, astrologer, rich man, merchant, knight, ploughman, child, … `[S2]` `[S3]`
- The **hourglass** recurs as Death's prop — **25 of 35 times**; broken crowns/swords = failed authority. `[S2]`
- Each block is tiny / palm-sized. `[model: needs-check]` — typical figure ~6.5 cm; not in fetched sources → keep vague ("smaller than a playing card") or drop.

## How Death behaves (the tonal engine)
- Death tailors the encounter to the victim: a **"dispenser of justice, denouncing greediness and the abuse of power."** `[S2]`
- Toward the powerful: nobility faces Death as **"invincible"**; the **rich man** is robbed of his **"money before stealing his soul"**; Death **"boldly pulls an abbot"** and **"skewers a knight."** `[S2]` `[S3]`
- Toward the **ploughman (laboureur)**: Death **"helps … to plough his field"** — assistant, not antagonist; "takes a gentler route when dealing with the less fortunate." `[S2]` `[S3]`  ← THE PIVOT
- Death **"kidnaps a child"** cruelly; **"solemnly attends"** the elderly to their rest. `[S2]`

## Interpretation (for the competing-theories beat)
- Traditional reading: **memento mori** + **Death as the great equalizer** — class distinction "completely neutralized." `[S1]`
- Reformist/social reading: a **"reformist satire"** of the late-medieval Danse Macabre, reflecting a shift toward reformed Christianity. `[S1]` The "social criticism of the wealthy and the Catholic church." `[S3]` (author's interpretation, flagged)
- NUANCE / counter-evidence: **"neither the preacher nor the priest attract Holbein's sarcasms"** — his lower clergy are shown sympathetic to the poor. `[S2]` → complicates a blanket "anticlerical" claim; use to keep ambiguity honest.
